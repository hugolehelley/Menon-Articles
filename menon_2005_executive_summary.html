Potency following robotic radical prostatectomy: conventional nerve sparing vs. prostatic fascia sparing

Background & rationale

Conventional nerve-sparing radical prostatectomy (RP) yields potency rates of 10%–86% — a wide range attributable to inconsistent potency definitions across studies.
The SHIM (Sexual Health Inventory for Men / IIEF-5) is a validated 5-item self-administered tool. A score >21 has 98% negative predictive value for ED; only 10–20% of post-RP patients reach this bar.
Research suggests accessory neural channels exist within the prostatic fascia — beyond the classic neurovascular bundles — that may contribute to penile innervation.
Menon's team developed the "Veil of Aphrodite" technique: preservation of the entire prostatic fascia during VIP (Vattikuti Institute Prostatectomy), hypothesizing enhanced nerve preservation via the da Vinci robot's stereoscopic magnification.

Study design & methods

Prospective, non-randomized Single surgeon (Menon). Eligibility: SHIM >21 without PDE5i (fully potent preoperatively).
Study group (Veil): Low-risk D'Amico disease (PSA ≤10, cT1c, Gleason ≤6) + 8 young intermediate-risk patients who elected the procedure.
Control group (conventional NS): All other potent patients — predominantly intermediate-risk (93%).
Primary endpoint: sexual intercourse at least once in the 4 weeks prior to questionnaire. Secondary endpoints: SHIM >21 with or without PDE5i; SHIM >21 without PDE5i. Follow-up: 12 months.
Key technical difference: In the Veil group, Hem-o-lok clips were not used; individual vessels entering the prostate were coagulated with bipolar forceps, and scissor dissection separated the fascia from the capsule.

Oncologic safety

Positive surgical margins: 1 (2%) in the Veil group (focal, apical) and 1 (3%) in the control group (posterolateral NVB) — no difference (p = 1.0). Both had undetectable PSA at 12 months. No positive margins in the veil region itself.

Authors' proposed mechanisms

Accessory nerve preservation: Prostatic fascia contains additional erectile nerve fibers beyond the classic NVBs; the Veil technique may spare them.
Reduced neuropraxia: A more distant dissection plane may minimize traction and thermal injury to the main NVBs.
Vascular preservation: Fascia preservation may maintain additional cavernous blood supply, supporting endothelial nitric oxide production — the key mediator of penile erection.

Limitations acknowledged by the authors

Bias risk Non-randomized; data collection/analysis done by team members directly involved in surgery — no independent third-party evaluator.
Selection Study group was younger (57.4 vs. 60.5 yrs, p=0.03) and predominantly low-risk disease; control group was 93% intermediate-risk.
Attrition Only 76% questionnaire response rate; nonresponders excluded. Worst-case scenario: potency 60% (control) vs. 74% (study).
PDE5i confound No standardized PDE5i rehabilitation protocol; frequency/duration of use not rigorously tracked — acknowledged by editorial commentator (Mulhall) as a critical gap.
Single surgeon All cases performed by Menon; results may not generalize. Larger multi-institutional validation needed.

Likely questions Dr. Menon may ask you

1

Why did we choose SHIM >21 as the eligibility threshold — and how does that affect the generalizability of these results?

Know that SHIM >21 = fully potent. Only 10–20% of all post-RP patients reach this in the general literature. Results are not applicable to men with preexisting ED.

2

The control group was 93% intermediate-risk vs. 17% in the study group. How does that affect your interpretation of the potency difference?

Patient selection bias is the central confound. Be ready to discuss D'Amico classification and why risk category affects surgical dissection extent.

3

What are the three hypothesized mechanisms for improved potency with the Veil technique — and which does the data in this paper actually support?

Accessory nerves, reduced neuropraxia/thermal injury, vascular preservation. The paper acknowledges no microdissection studies were done — none of the three is directly proven here.

4

Commentator Mulhall flagged PDE5i use as a critical variable. How should a future trial control for this?

Early penile rehabilitation (nightly PDE5i starting 4 weeks post-op) — cite Padma-Nathan's data on sildenafil and cavernous nerve recovery. Both groups in this study were not systematically managed.

5

We found no positive margins in the veil region. What is your concern about performing fascia preservation in intermediate or high-risk disease?

Extraprostatic extension risk. Fascia preservation requires the cancer to be confined; in higher-risk disease the veil may need to be sacrificed for oncologic control.

6

The accessory pudendal artery was not mentioned in our paper. Why might Mulhall raise this — and what is its relevance?

Accessory pudendal arteries contribute to cavernous arterial inflow. Control group may have had them sacrificed. The authors' reply: with 3-D robotic vision, these were easily identified and preserved in both groups.

7

If only 76% of patients returned questionnaires, what is the worst-case potency rate for the Veil group — and does it still support the technique?

Worst case (all non-responders impotent): 74% Veil vs. 60% control — still favors Veil, but the gap narrows significantly.

8

What study design would you recommend to definitively validate the Veil of Aphrodite technique?

Prospective randomized trial; matched for D'Amico risk, age, comorbidity severity; standardized PDE5i rehab; cavernous nerve functional assessment intraoperatively; independent third-party questionnaire administration; multi-institutional.
