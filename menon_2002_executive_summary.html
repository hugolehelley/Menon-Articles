Article 2 of 3 — Research Brief Foundational / Historical — 2002

Laparoscopic and robot assisted radical prostatectomy: establishment of a structured program and preliminary analysis of outcomes

Menon M, Shrivastava A, Tewari A, Sarle R, Hemal A, Peabody JO, Vallancien G

The Journal of Urology · Vol. 168, pp. 945–949 · September 2002 · Vattikuti Urology Institute, Henry Ford Health System + Institut Mutualiste Montsouris, Paris

Why this paper matters in context: This is the origin story. Published 3 years before the Veil of Aphrodite paper (Article 1), this is where Menon formally introduces the robotic prostatectomy program at VUI — before it had a name, before VIP was fully defined, and while the daVinci was still FDA-approved only months prior. Understanding this paper helps you trace how Menon's thinking evolved from "can we do this?" to "how do we do it better?"

The core hypothesis

American urologists were skeptical of laparoscopic RP in 2000: the procedure was considered too difficult, with an expected learning curve of 40–60 cases per surgeon, and many feared widespread adoption would generate unacceptable complication rates.
Two specific limitations of laparoscopic RP drove the robotic hypothesis: (1) lack of 3-D visualization and (2) rigid instruments limiting freedom of motion — particularly for dissection and vesicourethral anastomosis.
Menon's hypothesis: the daVinci's 3-D vision and wristed instruments could enable an experienced open surgeon (without laparoscopic experience) to achieve laparoscopic RP outcomes comparable to experts in the non-robotic technique, faster, and with a shorter learning curve.

The learning curve — the paper's most original contribution

Most prior publications defined learning curves arbitrarily — dividing cases into sequential cohorts without statistical justification. Menon's team instead used longitudinal regression analysis and computer-fitted power trend lines on operative time data.
RAP showed a strong, statistically significant decrease in operative time with experience (Spearman's Rho −0.72, p <0.0001). LRP operative times were stable across the series (Rho −0.21, p=0.18) — as expected, since LRP was performed by already-expert surgeons.
The power trendlines of both groups crossed at case 18 — meaning that by RAP case 18, Menon's robotic operative times were equivalent to the expert laparoscopic surgeons. This is a substantially shorter learning curve than the field's expected 40–60 cases.
Caveat: Menon had the advantage of being trained in open RP by Walsh and in laparoscopic RP by Guillonneau and Vallancien — arguably the three most prominent figures in the field. This extraordinary mentorship may not be replicable.

Functional outcomes (preliminary, immature)

Data explicitly described as not yet mature enough for meaningful analysis — mean follow-up was only 6.5 months (LRP) and 1.5 months (RAP).
Preliminary third-party telephone survey: potency returned to baseline in 3 of 12 LRP and 5 of 17 RAP patients. Continence (no pads): 20 of 20 in each group at the time of survey.
Overall satisfaction: 95% LRP and 91% RAP. Note that all 40 RAP cases were bilateral nerve-sparing, whereas LRP included 15 non–nerve-sparing and 6 unilateral cases — an important asymmetry when comparing functional outcomes.

Key limitations acknowledged

Non-randomized Patients declined randomization in a pilot survey. Unmeasured confounders (the authors humorously call this the "Gremlin factor") cannot be excluded.
Multi-surgeon Two surgeons (Guillonneau, Vallancien) performed most LRP; Menon performed most RAP. The study compared techniques, not surgeons, but was not powered to detect individual surgeon differences.
Exclusions 18 of 98 cases excluded: 8 LRP cases dropped due to AESOP camera voice-recognition system failures; 10 RAP cases dropped because they were performed primarily by the French surgeons rather than Menon. Selective exclusions require scrutiny.
Follow-up asymmetry Mean follow-up: 8.5 months for LRP vs. 3.0 months for RAP (p <0.001) — RAP data far too early for functional or oncologic conclusions.
Technique heterogeneity First 22 RAP cases used the Montsouris technique; last 18 used the nascent VIP technique. These are pooled together — the VIP cohort's results are not separately reported here.

Likely questions Dr. Menon may ask you

1

Why did we start a robotic program rather than simply adopt pure laparoscopic RP like the Europeans had done?

Two specific technical gaps: lack of 3-D visualization and loss of motion freedom with rigid instruments. Know that daVinci received FDA abdominal approval while the program was being designed — the timing was fortuitous.

2

What was novel about our method of analyzing the learning curve compared to what had been published before?

Prior studies arbitrarily split cases into cohorts. Menon's team used longitudinal regression and power trend lines on individual operative time data points — a more statistically rigorous, continuous approach. The trendlines crossing at case 18 is the key result.

3

We excluded 18 of 98 cases. Walk me through the reason for each group of exclusions and whether you think they were justified.

8 LRP: prolonged due to AESOP technical failures (defensible — equipment failure, not surgical). 10 RAP: performed primarily by French surgeons, not Menon — defensible since the hypothesis was about an "open" surgeon using the robot, but this cherry-picking of who "counts" deserves scrutiny.

4

The 40 RAP cases aren't actually a homogeneous group. What changed midway through, and why does that matter?

Cases 1–22 used Montsouris technique; cases 23–40 used the nascent VIP technique. The two techniques have different approaches, yet they're reported together. A skeptic would note that if VIP is superior (as later shown), pooling them understates VIP performance and overstates early RAP performance.

5

The blood loss was significantly lower in RAP (256 vs. 391 ml, p=0.013). What do you think explains that finding?

3-D visualization allows more precise identification and ligation of small vessels. Wristed instruments allow more delicate bipolar coagulation near the NVBs. Also: pneumoperitoneum pressure may reduce venous bleeding. Note that Hb drop (2.6 vs 3.0 g/dL) was not statistically significant — a clinically important nuance.

6

Can you tell me why the positive margin rates — 25% LRP vs. 17.5% RAP — were not statistically significant, and should we be reassured or concerned?

Small n (40 per group) — underpowered to detect a ~7% difference. Neither rate is alarming vs. the literature (Table 4 shows 13–26% across European centers). But the trend favoring RAP would later be borne out as VIP technique matured. Be ready to discuss the clinical significance of focal vs. extensive positive margins.

7

This paper was published in 2002. Knowing what you know from the 2005 Veil of Aphrodite paper, what did this early work fail to anticipate or measure?

No standardized nerve-sparing grading, no validated potency questionnaires (SHIM not systematically used), no 12-month functional outcomes, no distinction between Montsouris and VIP technique outcomes, no discussion of the prostatic fascia or accessory neural channels. This paper is a proof of feasibility; the 2005 paper asks what actually matters to patients.

8

Menon named the daVinci robot "Leonardo." The paper closes with an Aristotle quote. What does that tell you about how Menon viewed this work at the time?

A lighter question, but one that reveals character. The paper has a self-aware, even playful intellectual confidence — "one swallow does not a summer make" is a genuine epistemic humility statement. Be ready to engage with the idea that Menon saw this as a beginning, not a conclusion.
