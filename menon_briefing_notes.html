That's your prep brief for Article 1. A few things to keep at the front of your mind walking into this meeting: The paper's central claim — that the Veil of Aphrodite technique produces dramatically better potency outcomes — is well-supported by the numbers, but the non-randomized design with significant baseline differences (risk stratification, age) between groups is the study's Achilles heel. Menon knows this and acknowledges it; he will likely probe whether you can identify it independently. The editorial comment by Mulhall from Weill Cornell is unusually pointed for a journal comment. Reading it carefully and being able to articulate both Mulhall's critique and the authors' reply signals that you engaged with the full paper, not just the abstract — that will make a strong impression. Finally, because all surgeries were performed by a single surgeon who also developed the technique and analyzed the data, the conflict-of-interest dimension is worth understanding. Dr. Menon is intellectually honest about this in the discussion, but he will likely appreciate a resident who can engage with the rigor question directly.

Article 2

Article 2 is prepped. A few things worth internalizing before the meeting: This paper sits at a genuinely historic moment — the first year of robotic prostatectomy at an American institution, with the daVinci barely FDA-approved. Menon is essentially betting his surgical reputation on an unproven platform, and the paper reads like someone who knows it. The intellectual honesty is notable: he names the unmeasured canfounder the "Gremlin factor," acknowledges the learning curve advantage may be unique to his training background, and explicitly says functional data are too immature to report. That candor is characteristic of Menon's writing across all three articles. The thread connecting this paper to Article 1 (2005) is important to hold in your head: the 2002 paper proves robotic RP is feasible and safe. The 2005 paper asks the next-level question — can we do it in a way that produces better functional outcomes than even the best conventional approach? Menon will likely appreciate a resident who can articulate that intellectual progression.

Closing Thoughts

A few closing thoughts to carry into the meeting: The three papers together tell a coherent nine-year story. In 2002, Menon is an open surgeon picking up a robot and proving it can work. By 2005, he's redesigning the anatomical approach to see if robotics can yield better functional outcomes than conventional technique. By 2011, his program has 5,000+ cases and is publishing the most rigorous safety analysis in the RARP literature. If Menon asks you a broad question about the arc of his work, that narrative is the right frame. The methodological thread across all three papers is also worth internalizing: each one confronts a reporting problem in the prior literature — inconsistent potency definitions in 2005, arbitrary learning-curve analysis in 2002, unstandardized complication capture in 2011. Menon is consistently interested in whether the field is measuring the right things the right way, not just in whether his numbers are good. That epistemological instinct is likely something he'll want to discuss. Finally, the question that bridges all three papers is the one that remains open: there is still no large, multi-institutional randomized trial comparing RARP to open RP on long-term trifecta outcomes. Knowing that gap exists — and being able to articulate why it's hard to close — will signal that you've read these papers as a researcher, not just a student. Good luck today.
